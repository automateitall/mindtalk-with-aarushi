People often tell me they "just need a good weekend" — and then the weekend comes and goes, and they still feel drained on Monday morning. That gap, between resting and actually recovering, is usually the first sign that what's happening is burnout rather than ordinary tiredness.

## Burnout isn't a character flaw

One of the most common things I hear from clients is some version of "I should be able to handle this." Burnout doesn't mean you're weak or lack resilience — it means you've been running on a deficit for long enough that your usual coping isn't covering the gap anymore. It's a sign the demands have outpaced the resources, not a verdict on your character.

## The quiet signs

Burnout often builds slowly, so it can be hard to notice from the inside. A few things worth paying attention to:

- Dread creeping in before the week has even started
- Feeling cynical or detached about work you used to care about
- Concentration that used to come easily now feels like wading through fog
- Losing interest in things outside work that used to matter to you

## Why pushing harder backfires

The instinct, when you're behind, is usually to push harder — more hours, more effort, more willpower. But burnout is often *caused* by exactly that pattern. Pushing through it tends to deepen it, not resolve it. What actually helps is figuring out what's driving it — the pressures, the boundaries, the patterns — and making sustainable changes, not just gritting your teeth for another quarter.

If any of this sounds familiar, it's worth taking seriously rather than waiting for it to pass on its own.
